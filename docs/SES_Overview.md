# Sensory Electrical Stimulation (SES) — Overview and Clinical Background

Sensory Electrical Stimulation (SES) is a non-invasive neuromodulation technique that delivers low-level electrical currents through the skin at intensities below the motor threshold — providing sensory feedback without causing muscles to twitch or contract. This project uses SES to target **Essential Tremor (ET)**, the most common movement disorder in older adults, characterised by a rhythmic action/postural tremor of the hands at 5–8 Hz.

> SES has also been studied as a therapy for Parkinson's disease (PD) tremor, and the TremorBump device could in principle be applied there as well. However, ET is the focus of this project.

## How SES Works

Surface electrodes are placed over major nerves at the wrist (median and radial nerves) or over motor points on the forearm muscles. These electrodes deliver electrical pulses — often organised into high-frequency bursts — to disrupt pathological signals in the Cerebello-Thalamo-Cortical (CTC) circuit, which governs tremor amplitude in ET. By sending artificial sensory signals into this circuit, SES can "jam" or dephase the rhythmic oscillations that drive the involuntary hand movements.

## Expected Sensation

The patient should feel a **strong but comfortable tingling or tickling** sensation in the hand or fingers. Intensity must be set individually to stay at this sensory level — it should never cause an electric shock sensation or visible muscle contraction.

## Therapy Sessions

| Parameter | Typical value |
|---|---|
| Session length | 20–40 minutes |
| Frequency | 3× per week to daily |
| Duration of effect | Immediate; can persist >1 hour after stimulation ends |

Repeated daily use over months does not appear to lead to a loss of the lasting post-stimulation effect.

For the full technical detail — fiber types, electrode landmarks, stimulation parameters, and clinical outcomes — see [`SES_Clinical_Reference.md`](SES_Clinical_Reference.md).

## Estimated Efficacy

The figures below come primarily from studies on PD and general tremor populations, as large-scale ET-specific SES trials remain limited. The underlying mechanism (CTC circuit modulation) is shared with ET, so the results are considered indicative.

| Scenario | Tremor reduction |
|---|---|
| General SES | 35–48% average reduction |
| Calibrated therapies (e.g. TAPS) | >90% of patients see some reduction; majority achieve ≥50% decrease in tremor power |
| Phase-locked stimulation, severe tremors | 60–65% reduction in tremor power |
| Combined with physical therapy, suitable responders | Up to 83% average magnitude decrease |
